# Is Your $400 CAC Good?

Short answer: **Yes, your $400 CAC is solid — but there's room to push further.**

## The Math

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Monthly Revenue per Customer | $50 |
| Average Customer Lifetime | 18 months |
| Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) | **$900** |
| Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) | $400 |
| LTV:CAC Ratio | **2.25:1** |
| CAC Payback Period | **8 months** |

## What the Numbers Mean

**LTV:CAC Ratio of 2.25:1**
The general benchmark for healthy SaaS/subscription businesses is **3:1 or higher**. At 2.25:1, you're profitable on each customer — you make $500 gross profit per acquisition — but you're below the standard target. Investors and growth operators typically want to see 3:1 before aggressively scaling spend.

**CAC Payback Period of 8 months**
You recoup your acquisition cost in 8 months, then have 10 months of margin. This is reasonable. Under 12 months is generally considered acceptable; under 6 months is excellent.

## Verdict

- **Not bad** — you're cash-flow positive per customer and the unit economics work.
- **Not great** — the 2.25:1 ratio is below the 3:1 benchmark, meaning capital efficiency could be better.
- **Actionable**: To hit a 3:1 ratio with your current $50/month pricing and 18-month retention, you'd want to bring CAC down to ~$300, or increase LTV to $1,200 (e.g., retain customers to 24 months or raise ARPU to ~$67/month).

## How to Improve

1. **Reduce CAC**: Invest in lower-cost acquisition channels (SEO, referrals, content) to bring average CAC down.
2. **Extend retention**: Even moving average lifetime from 18 to 22 months raises LTV to $1,100 and your ratio to 2.75:1.
3. **Increase ARPU**: Upsells, add-ons, or price increases on the existing base improve LTV without touching CAC.
4. **Segment your CAC**: Your average may be masking a mix — some channels may already be at 3:1+ while others drag the average down. Focus on the efficient channels.

## Bottom Line

Your business is **fundamentally healthy** at these numbers — every customer you acquire is profitable. But to scale confidently and attract investment, closing the gap to a 3:1 LTV:CAC ratio should be a near-term priority.
