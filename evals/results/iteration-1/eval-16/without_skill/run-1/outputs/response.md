# Win-Back Email Sequence: 60-Day Inactive Customers

## Overview

This 5-email sequence targets customers who have not logged in for 60 days. The goal is to re-engage them, remind them of your product's value, address potential friction, and recover their active use before they churn permanently.

**Sequence Duration:** 30 days (Day 60 through Day 90 of inactivity)
**Trigger:** Customer has not logged in for exactly 60 days

---

## Email 1 — "We Miss You" (Day 60 of Inactivity)

**Subject Line Options:**
- "It's been a while, [First Name] — here's what you've missed"
- "Still there, [First Name]? We saved your spot"
- "[First Name], your account is waiting for you"

**Goal:** Soft re-engagement. Remind them the account exists and hint at value.

**Body:**

> Hi [First Name],
>
> We noticed you haven't stopped by in about two months — life gets busy, we get it.
>
> Your account is exactly where you left it. Everything is saved, your settings are intact, and [key feature] is ready whenever you are.
>
> Since you were last here, we've shipped a few things you might like:
> - [New Feature 1]
> - [New Feature 2]
> - [Improvement relevant to their use case]
>
> Ready to pick up where you left off?
>
> [CTA Button: Jump Back In]
>
> — [Team Name]

**Design Notes:** Keep it warm and low-pressure. No urgency language. Include a screenshot or GIF of one compelling new feature.

---

## Email 2 — Value Reminder + Social Proof (Day 67, +7 Days)

**Subject Line Options:**
- "What customers like you are accomplishing with [Product]"
- "Here's what's possible — come see for yourself"
- "Your peers are doing this with [Product]"

**Goal:** Re-establish value and reduce fear of returning. Use peer proof.

**Body:**

> Hi [First Name],
>
> We wanted to share what customers in your space are doing with [Product] right now:
>
> "[Customer quote about a specific outcome]" — [Name, Role, Company]
>
> They [achieved specific result] in [timeframe]. You set up your account for the same use case.
>
> Coming back takes less than a minute. Your data and history are still here.
>
> [CTA Button: See What's New]
>
> If you've hit a snag or something isn't working for you, just reply to this email. We read every response.

**Design Notes:** Include one short customer success story or stat. Keep the CTA light ("See What's New" vs. "Log In").

---

## Email 3 — Remove Friction / Offer Help (Day 74, +14 Days)

**Subject Line Options:**
- "Is something getting in the way, [First Name]?"
- "We want to make [Product] work for you — can we help?"
- "Not a fit? We'd rather know."

**Goal:** Address potential blockers (confusion, a bad experience, a competitor). Invite honest feedback. Offer 1:1 help.

**Body:**

> Hi [First Name],
>
> I'll be direct: you haven't logged in for a couple of months and I want to understand why.
>
> Is it one of these?
> - Things got busy and you just haven't had a chance
> - Something wasn't working the way you expected
> - You're not sure how to get the most out of [Product]
> - You've moved to something else
>
> Any of the above is okay to say. If you're stuck or lost, I'd personally like to help. Just reply with what's going on and I'll make sure you get what you need — whether that's a 15-minute walkthrough, better documentation, or something else entirely.
>
> And if [Product] isn't the right fit, I'd still love to hear why. It helps us build something better.
>
> [CTA Button: Book a 15-Minute Call] or [Reply to This Email]
>
> — [Name], Customer Success

**Design Notes:** Send from a named person (ideally Customer Success). Plain-text style works well here to feel personal. Two CTAs: async (reply) and sync (call booking).

---

## Email 4 — Incentive Offer (Day 81, +21 Days)

**Subject Line Options:**
- "A little something to welcome you back, [First Name]"
- "We'd like to give you [offer] — no strings"
- "[First Name], here's an exclusive offer just for you"

**Goal:** Provide a concrete reason to return now. Limited-time framing adds urgency without being aggressive.

**Body:**

> Hi [First Name],
>
> We haven't heard from you, so we'd like to make it easier to come back.
>
> For the next 7 days, we're offering you:
>
> **[Offer — choose one that fits your model:]**
> - One month free / extended trial
> - 30% off your next billing cycle
> - Free access to [premium feature] for 30 days
> - A free onboarding session with our team
>
> No hoops. Just click below, log in, and the offer is applied automatically.
>
> [CTA Button: Claim My Offer — Expires [Date]]
>
> This offer expires on [date]. After that it's gone, but your account will still be here.

**Design Notes:** Be specific about the offer and the expiry date. Bold the offer. Keep everything else minimal so the offer is the focus.

---

## Email 5 — Final Goodbye / Account Closure Notice (Day 88, +28 Days)

**Subject Line Options:**
- "We're about to close your [Product] account, [First Name]"
- "Last chance — your account will be deactivated on [date]"
- "Should we delete your account, [First Name]?"

**Goal:** Create genuine urgency. Some customers will re-engage just to avoid losing their data/progress. Others will unsubscribe, which is a healthy list hygiene outcome.

**Body:**

> Hi [First Name],
>
> This is a heads-up that we'll be deactivating inactive accounts on [date — 7 days from send].
>
> Your account is scheduled to be closed on that date unless you log in before then.
>
> If you'd like to keep your account — along with all your saved data, settings, and history — just log in once before [date].
>
> [CTA Button: Keep My Account — Log In Now]
>
> If you're ready to move on, no action needed. We'll take care of it, and there are no hard feelings.
>
> Thanks for giving [Product] a try.
>
> — [Team Name]

**Design Notes:** Only use this email if you are actually willing to deactivate accounts — do not make a false threat. If deactivation is not feasible, reframe as "we'll archive your data" or omit this email. The subject line is intentionally alarming; A/B test against a softer version.

---

## Sequence Summary

| # | Send Timing | Subject Theme | Primary CTA |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Day 60 | We miss you + what's new | Log back in |
| 2 | Day 67 | Social proof + value reminder | See what's new |
| 3 | Day 74 | Remove friction / offer help | Reply or book a call |
| 4 | Day 81 | Incentive offer | Claim offer |
| 5 | Day 88 | Final warning / account closure | Keep my account |

---

## Segmentation Recommendations

- **Paid vs. Free:** Send the incentive offer only to paid (or previously paid) customers. For free-tier users, replace Email 4 with an upgrade offer or skip it.
- **Early-stage users (< 3 logins total):** Front-load help and onboarding content. These customers likely never found value — focus Email 2 and 3 on education.
- **Power users who went quiet:** Prioritize the "what's new" angle. They knew the product and left for a reason — new features or fixes are your best hook.
- **Enterprise accounts:** Suppress from automated sequence; route to account manager for direct outreach instead.

---

## Exit Conditions (Stop Sending When:)

- Customer logs in at any point
- Customer unsubscribes
- Customer replies to any email (route to human)
- Account is formally cancelled

---

## Metrics to Track

- **Open rate** by email number (expect drop-off; Email 5 should spike)
- **Click-to-login rate** (primary conversion metric)
- **Reply rate** on Email 3 (signals engaged but stuck customers)
- **Offer redemption rate** on Email 4
- **Reactivation rate** (logged in and took a meaningful action within 7 days of returning)
- **Unsubscribe rate** (healthy to see here; cleans your list)

---

## Assumptions Made

1. You have email permission for these customers (they opted in at signup).
2. You can track login activity and trigger sequences based on it.
3. "Win-back" means reactivating dormant users, not recovering cancelled/churned paying customers (the sequence above covers dormant users; a cancelled-account sequence would differ).
4. You have the ability to offer at least one of the incentives listed in Email 4.
5. Account deactivation in Email 5 is real or can be framed as data archival — adjust if not.
