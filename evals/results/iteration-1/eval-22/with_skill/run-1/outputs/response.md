# How to Get Your First 100 Customers

Before I dive in: the tactics below are sequenced for an early-stage online store with no audience yet and a limited budget. They will work for most product categories, but if you tell me what you sell and who your ideal buyer is, I can sharpen the priorities significantly — some of this will be far more relevant to you than the rest.

---

## The core principle for getting to 100

Don't try to be everywhere. Pick **one channel and one motion**, execute it until it works, then layer on a second. The number-one mistake new stores make is dabbling in five channels simultaneously and doing none of them well enough to see results. Focus beats breadth at this stage.

Your job at this stage is not to "do marketing." It is to find your first buyers, learn what message resonates, and collect proof (reviews, testimonials, before/after) so that everything after 100 gets easier.

---

## Step 1 — Make sure your store can convert before you drive traffic

No amount of traffic fixes a store that can't convert. Before you spend a minute on acquisition:

- **Product page**: Does the headline say exactly what the product does and for whom? Is there at least one photo that shows the product in real use? Is the price and shipping cost visible before checkout?
- **Trust signals**: Do you have any reviews, a clear returns policy, and contact information? Without these, first-time visitors bounce.
- **Mobile**: Pull it up on your phone. If it's slow or hard to navigate, fix it first.

If these aren't solid, your acquisition effort will leak.

---

## Step 2 — Choose your first channel based on where your buyer already is

Answer this honestly: **Where does your ideal customer spend time when they're looking for what you sell?**

| If your buyer... | Start here |
|---|---|
| Searches Google/Amazon for your product type | SEO + organic search (Google Shopping, Amazon if applicable) |
| Hangs out in specific communities (Reddit, Facebook Groups, Discord, forums) | Community seeding + word of mouth |
| Follows creators or influencers in your niche | Micro-influencer outreach |
| Is a local buyer or attends events | Local presence + in-person |
| Doesn't know they need your product yet | Content that introduces the problem |

Pick the one that fits best. That is your primary channel for the next 60 days.

---

## Step 3 — The fastest paths to your first 100 (ranked by speed and cost)

### A. Your personal network — underused, free, fast
Tell every person you know what you've built and ask them directly: "Would you buy this? Do you know anyone who would?" Don't hint. Ask directly. Post about it on your personal social accounts with a specific ask ("I just launched X — if you've ever struggled with Y, check it out and share it if you know anyone").

One honest post to 200 Facebook friends or LinkedIn connections can produce 5–15 sales and several warm referrals. Most founders skip this because it feels awkward. Do it anyway.

### B. Relevant communities — free, medium speed
Find 3–5 communities where your target buyer is active: Reddit, Facebook Groups, niche forums, Slack/Discord communities, etc. **Do not spam.** Instead:

1. Spend one week genuinely contributing — answering questions, adding value, being a real member.
2. Then, when it's natural (or in communities that allow it, in a "share your project" post), introduce your store in context: "I built X because I kept running into problem Y — happy to share a discount code with anyone who wants to try it."

This builds real buyers and real feedback, not vanity traffic.

### C. Micro-influencer outreach — low cost, medium speed
Find 10–20 creators in your niche with 1,000–20,000 followers and a genuinely engaged audience (check: do real people comment on their posts?). Send a short, direct DM or email:

> "Hi [name], I sell [product] and think your audience would genuinely like it. Would you be open to trying it for free and sharing your honest take if you like it?"

No payment required at this stage. Most will ignore you; 1–3 will say yes. One good micro-influencer post can deliver 20–50 buyers, with social proof you can reuse forever.

### D. Organic social content — free, slow but compounds
If you have time and enjoy creating content, post consistently on the platform where your buyer hangs out (Instagram, TikTok, Pinterest, YouTube Shorts are all viable depending on your product). The content formula that works for early stores:

- Show the product in real, authentic use (not polished ads)
- Show the problem it solves, then the solution
- Behind-the-scenes: how it's made, who's behind it, the "why"

Aim for 3–5 posts per week, not 1 per day. Consistency over volume. Expect 4–8 weeks before you see real traction.

### E. Paid ads — fast, but risky before you have proof
Facebook/Instagram or Google ads can work, but they are expensive to learn. Don't start here unless you have:
- At least a few real customer reviews
- A clear, tested message (you know what makes people buy)
- Budget to spend $500–$1,000 learning before expecting returns

If you don't have those yet, earn them through the free channels first.

## Step 4 — Build an email list from day one

Every visitor who doesn't buy is gone forever unless you capture them. Set up a simple email capture on your store (a popup or a banner offering a small discount or a helpful resource) before you drive any meaningful traffic. Even 10 subscribers a week compounds into an asset.

When someone does buy: email them to say thank you, ask for a review, and invite them to share. Word of mouth at this stage is disproportionately powerful — one happy customer who tells two friends is worth more than a paid ad.

---

## Step 5 — Track one number per week

Don't try to measure everything. For the next 60 days, track only:

1. **Visitors** — how many people are landing on your store?
2. **Conversion rate** — what % are buying? (Typical e-commerce is 1–3%; below 0.5% means something on the store is broken)
3. **Source** — where did each sale come from?

Source data tells you which channel is working. Double down on that one. Cut the rest.

## The 60-day sequence

| Week | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1 | Fix store (trust signals, mobile, product page). Tell your network. |
| 2–3 | Join and contribute to 3–5 communities. Set up email capture. Begin micro-influencer outreach. |
| 4–6 | Post content consistently on one platform. Follow up with influencers. Ask first buyers for reviews. |
| 7–8 | Double down on what's producing sales. Consider a small paid test if you have 10+ reviews. |
