# Value Proposition

*Assuming your core audience is parents of children under 18 who are time-poor on weeknights and already
skeptical of "healthy" convenience food that turns out to be full of additives. Adjust if wrong.*

---

## Primary Value Proposition

**Real food on the table in 5 minutes — no shortcuts, no junk.**

> For busy parents who don't have time to cook from scratch but refuse to feed their kids ultra-processed
> food, [Brand] is a meal-prep service that puts a real, whole-ingredient dinner on the table in
> 5 minutes flat — so you never have to choose between time and what's actually in the food.

---

## Expanded version (for a homepage hero or About section)

**You shouldn't have to choose between fast and real.**

Most "convenient" meal options buy you time by trading away ingredients — fillers, preservatives,
flavour enhancers you'd rather not read aloud. [Brand] does neither.

Every meal ships prepped and portioned with whole, recognisable ingredients. Heat it, plate it, sit
down — 5 minutes, start to finish. No ultra-processed anything. No compromise.

**Fast food for parents who know better.**

## Tagline options

| Angle | Tagline |
|---|---|
| Speed + integrity | **Real food. 5 minutes. No asterisks.** |
| Parent-focused outcome | **Dinner done before the argument starts.** |
| Direct differentiator | **Convenient. Clean. Actually 5 minutes.** |

---

## Why this works

- Leads with the tension the audience actually feels (time vs. ingredient quality), not a product
  feature list.
- "5 minutes" is a concrete, verifiable claim — stronger than "quick" or "easy."
- "No ultra-processed ingredients" is a meaningful negative claim that busy parents who've checked
  labels will recognise and trust.
- The tagline options each take a distinct angle so you can test which resonates with your audience.

---

## To sharpen further, it would help to know:

1. **What's the main alternative parents are currently using?** (home cooking they don't have time for,
   meal-kit services, takeaway, supermarket ready meals) — the value prop lands harder when it's
   explicitly positioned against what the reader is doing today.
2. **Do you have any proof points?** (e.g. "used by 2,000 families", a specific ingredient standard
   you meet, a time guarantee) — one concrete proof element would significantly strengthen trust.
3. **What age range of children?** Toddler parents and parents of teenagers have different emotional
   triggers; the copy can be tuned accordingly.
