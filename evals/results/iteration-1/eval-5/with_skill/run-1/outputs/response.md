Defining your brand voice well requires a few inputs — brand voice is a strategy foundation that everything downstream (copy, campaigns, content) will inherit. A generic voice definition looks plausible but does more harm than good because it fits any brand.

Before I build yours, three quick questions:

1. **Who exactly is your core audience?** Be as specific as you can — role, situation, what they care about. (e.g. "solo freelance designers who hate chasing invoices" or "HR managers at 200-person SaaS companies dealing with high turnover"). Not a broad demographic — the narrowest real person you're writing for.

2. **What's your brand's single most important personality trait — and what's the one thing you're definitely NOT?** For example: "We're direct, never corporate" or "We're warm and human, not slick or salesy." If you have a gut sense of the vibe you want, even rough language helps (e.g. "smart friend," "no-nonsense expert," "encouraging coach").

3. **What are you — a product, a service, a personal brand, a startup?** And what's the category (e.g. fintech app, marketing agency, health coaching, B2B software)? Knowing the landscape helps calibrate what "distinctive" sounds like for you versus your competitors.

Once I have these, I'll build you a complete brand voice profile: 3–4 voice dimensions with "we are / we're not" definitions, do/don't examples for each, tone guidance for key moments (sales, support, social, bad news), and practical style rules (contractions, POV, vocabulary to use and avoid).
