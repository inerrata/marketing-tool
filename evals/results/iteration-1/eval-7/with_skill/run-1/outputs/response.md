# B2B Fintech Content Calendar

Before I build this, three quick questions would sharpen it considerably:

1. **Who exactly is the buyer?** (e.g., CFOs at mid-market companies, AP/AR managers at SMBs, CTOs at lending firms — the narrower the better)
2. **What's the one thing you do that alternatives don't?** (e.g., real-time reconciliation in 60 seconds, embedded compliance checks, open-banking API with 3-day go-live)
3. **Where are you in the funnel right now?** (Building brand awareness from scratch, or converting a warm pipeline that already knows you exist?)

If any of those change the picture, swap out the assumptions below. Otherwise, here's a full working calendar.

---

**Assumptions:** Audience = finance leaders (CFOs, Controllers, VP Finance) at B2B companies with 50–500 employees; product = a fintech platform solving cash-flow visibility or payments automation; company is in early-to-mid growth stage (seed to Series A/B); primary channels = LinkedIn, company blog/SEO, and email newsletter; goal = generate qualified pipeline (demo requests, newsletter sign-ups). Adjust if wrong.

---

## Content Strategy Foundation

### The Three Content Pillars

Before the calendar: every piece maps to one of three pillars. If it doesn't, cut it.

| Pillar | What it does | Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Category Education** | Teaches the problem — not your product. Builds credibility with buyers who don't yet know they need you. | 50% |
| **2. Proof & Validation** | Customer stories, data, third-party validation. Converts interested buyers into confident ones. | 30% |
| **3. Product & Company** | Product updates, company milestones, hiring, thought leadership from founders. | 20% |

Most early-stage fintechs invert this ratio — 80% product, 20% everything else — and wonder why their content doesn't convert. Buyers trust companies that teach first, sell second.

## 4-Week Rolling Content Calendar

This is designed as a repeating module. Run it for a quarter, then review what drove demo requests and double down.

| Day | Channel | Format | Topic/Angle | Pillar | CTA |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mon | LinkedIn | Long-form post (300–500 words) | "The hidden cost of manual payment reconciliation" — quantify the problem (hours lost, error rate, late fees) | 1 — Education | Comment with your current process |

| Thu | Email newsletter | Short-form (400 words) | Curated: 3 things finance leaders read this week + one original take from your team | 1 — Education | Reply with what you're watching |
| Fri | LinkedIn | Behind-the-scenes / founder post | What we got wrong in Q1 and what we're changing — honest, specific | 3 — Company | Tag a colleague who'd find this useful |

## Content Specifications by Channel

### LinkedIn (3–4x/week)
- **Best post formats for B2B fintech:** data posts, contrarian takes, short case studies, carousels
- **Optimal length:** 150–400 words. Hook in line 1 (before "see more"). No links in the post body — drop links in the first comment.
- **Posting time:** Tuesday–Thursday, 7:30–9:00 AM in your target audience's timezone
- **Engagement signal to optimize for:** Comments > Shares > Reactions. If you're getting only reactions, your posts aren't provocative enough.

### Blog / SEO (1x/week)
- Target one primary keyword per post. For early-stage, go after long-tail keywords with clear commercial intent (e.g., "accounts payable automation for mid-market" not "fintech").
- Every post needs: a clear H1, a meta description under 155 characters, internal links to 2–3 related posts, one conversion point (download, demo CTA, or newsletter sign-up).
- Repurpose every blog post into: 1 LinkedIn post, 1 newsletter section, and 3 pull-quote social images.

### Email Newsletter (1x/week)
- Send on Thursday morning. B2B open rates peak Tuesday–Thursday; Thursday gives you a less crowded inbox.
- Subject line formula: be specific and useful. "3 ways to reduce DSO" outperforms "Our monthly newsletter."
- Keep it to 400–600 words. One primary CTA per email. Track click-through rate on that CTA — it's your conversion signal.

## Quarterly Content Themes

Layer these themes over the 4-week rolling calendar to give each quarter a narrative arc:

| Quarter | Theme | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 (Jan–Mar) | **"The cost of the status quo"** | Start of the fiscal year — buyers are setting budgets and auditing what's broken |
| Q2 (Apr–Jun) | **"How high-performing finance teams operate"** | Mid-year review season — aspirational content resonates |
| Q3 (Jul–Sep) | **"Build vs. buy vs. automate"** | Budget planning season starts — catch buyers before they lock in decisions |
| Q4 (Oct–Dec) | **"Close the year strong / start next year right"** | Year-end urgency + forward-looking planning = high engagement |

## Content Governance

A calendar is only useful if it ships. Three things that kill B2B content programs:

1. **No single owner.** Assign one person who is responsible for the calendar, not just "marketing."
2. **Writing from scratch every week.** Build a content bank: every customer call, sales call, and support ticket is a content idea. Log them in a shared doc.
3. **Measuring vanity metrics.** Track: demo requests sourced from content, newsletter subscriber growth rate, and blog-to-demo conversion rate. Not follower count.

## 30-Day Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Confirm the three content pillars match your brand positioning
- [ ] Identify 2–3 customers who can provide a quote or participate in a case study
- [ ] Set up UTM tracking for all content CTAs so you can attribute demos
- [ ] Create a content idea log (a shared doc or Notion page is fine)
- [ ] Write Week 1 posts before Week 1 starts — never write the day of
- [ ] Establish a review process: who approves before publishing? (Keep it to one person)
- [ ] Set a 30-day review date to check what drove engagement and demo requests
