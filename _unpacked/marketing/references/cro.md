## CRO mindset

- **Optimize for the visitor's decision, not your preference.** CRO is applied empathy + evidence, not
  guessing what looks nice.
- **Test, don't assume.** Opinions about what will convert are frequently wrong; let data decide on
  anything high-stakes.
- **Bigger swings, bigger learning.** Testing button colors rarely moves the needle. Test messaging,
  offers, page structure, and value communication — that's where the gains are.
- **One change at a time** (in a clean A/B test) so you know what caused the result.

## Conversion audit

When auditing a page/funnel for conversion (no testing required to start):

1. **Clarity** — within 5 seconds, is it clear what this is, who it's for, and what to do? Confusion kills
   conversion faster than anything.
2. **Value communication** — is the benefit/outcome front and center, or buried under features?
3. **Relevance** — does the page match the source (the ad/email/link that brought them)? Message-match
   matters; a mismatch spikes bounce.
4. **Friction** — count the steps, fields, and decisions required. Each one leaks conversions. What can be
   removed, deferred, or simplified?
5. **Trust** — is there proof (testimonials, logos, guarantees, security signals) at the moments of doubt?
6. **CTA** — is there one clear primary action? Is it specific and visible? Are competing CTAs splitting
   attention?
7. **Objections** — are the real reasons people hesitate surfaced and answered?
8. **Friction-to-value ratio** — is the ask proportional to the perceived value and trust built so far?

Output: a prioritized list of fixes (highest impact / lowest effort first), not just observations.

## The conversion equation

A useful mental model: conversion happens when **motivation + incentive + clarity > friction + anxiety.**

To increase conversion, you can:
- **Increase motivation** — sharpen the value, target higher-intent traffic.
- **Increase clarity** — make the offer and action obvious.
- **Add incentive** — offer, urgency, guarantee.
- **Reduce friction** — fewer steps/fields, easier process.
- **Reduce anxiety** — trust signals, risk reversal, reassurance at decision points.

Diagnose which side of the equation is the bottleneck before changing things.

## What to test (in rough order of impact)

1. **Value proposition / headline** — how you communicate the core value. Usually the highest-impact test.
2. **Offer** — what you're offering, pricing/packaging, guarantee, bonus.
3. **Page structure / message order** — what you lead with, how you sequence the argument.
4. **Social proof** — type, placement, and prominence of proof.
5. **CTA** — copy, placement, prominence, number.
6. **Form/flow** — number of fields, steps, friction reducers.
7. **Visuals/media** — hero image vs. video, product demonstration.
8. **Microcopy** — reassurances near the CTA, form helper text.

Color/button tweaks are last — real but small.

## How to run an A/B test

1. **Form a hypothesis** — "Because [evidence], changing [element] to [variant] will [expected effect]
   measured by [metric]." A test without a hypothesis is just a coin flip.
2. **Change one variable** (for a clean A/B). Multivariate needs more traffic.
3. **Pick the primary metric** tied to business value (not a vanity metric).
4. **Determine sample size / duration** — run until statistically meaningful and across full business
   cycles (at least one to two weeks to cover weekday/weekend variation). Don't peek-and-stop early; that
   produces false positives.
5. **Run, then analyze** — did it reach significance? What's the lift? Segment results (did it help one
   audience but hurt another?).
6. **Decide and document** — ship the winner, log the learning (losers teach too), generate the next
   hypothesis.

Caveat to flag: low-traffic sites can't run reliable A/B tests (insufficient sample). For those, rely on
heuristic audits, qualitative research (session recordings, user testing, surveys), and best practices
rather than statistically dubious tests.

## Common high-impact fixes

- Replace a vague aspirational headline with a clear, specific value statement.
- Move proof higher (social proof near the hero and near CTAs).
- Cut form fields to the essential minimum; ask for more later.
- Add a risk reversal (guarantee, free trial, "cancel anytime").
- Make the single primary CTA unmistakable; demote or remove competing actions.
- Add message-match between ad/email and landing page.
- Answer the top 2–3 objections directly on the page.
- Reduce steps to the value/conversion moment.

## Friction & trust

**Friction** is anything that makes acting harder: too many fields, unclear next steps, slow load, forced
account creation, surprise costs, too many choices. Audit relentlessly and remove what isn't essential.

**Anxiety** is doubt that makes acting scarier: "will this work for me?", "is my data safe?", "can I get a
refund?", "is this legit?". Counter with proof, guarantees, security signals, transparent pricing, and
reassuring microcopy placed exactly where the doubt arises (usually at the form and the buy button).
