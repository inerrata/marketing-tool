## Email strategy principles

- **Email is owned audience** — the highest-leverage channel because you control it (no algorithm). Building
  and nurturing a list should be a priority in almost every program.
- **Every email needs one job** — one primary CTA. Multiple competing asks reduce action.
- **Value before asks** — especially in nurture. Earn attention by being useful, then you've earned the
  occasional sell.
- **Segment and personalize** — relevant beats clever. Send based on behavior, stage, and interest, not
  one blast to everyone.
- **Sequences > one-offs for key moments** — a planned sequence (welcome, launch, onboarding) outperforms
  ad-hoc sends because it builds narrative and momentum.

## Welcome sequence

The most important sequence — open rates are highest right after signup. Triggered on subscribe.

**Typical 3–5 email flow:**
1. **Deliver + welcome** (immediate) — deliver what they signed up for (lead magnet/confirmation), set
   expectations (what you'll send, how often), one clear next step. Warm, human.
2. **Story / why** (day 1–2) — who you are, why you exist, what you believe. Builds connection.
3. **Best value** (day 3–4) — your single most useful resource or insight. Prove the relationship is worth
   it.
4. **Social proof** (day 5–6) — results, testimonials, what's possible. Build belief.
5. **Soft offer** (day 7+) — the first invitation to take a commercial step, framed around their goal.

Goal: turn a subscriber into an engaged, trusting reader who's primed to eventually buy.

## Nurture sequence

For subscribers not yet ready to buy. Ongoing or fixed-length. Goal: stay top-of-mind, build trust, move
toward readiness.

- Lead with value (education, insight, useful resources) tied to your pillars.
- Weave in proof and product naturally — show the product solving the problems you teach about.
- Use behavioral triggers: clicked a topic → send more on that topic; visited pricing → send a
  consideration-stage email.
- Pace sells around value: roughly value-value-value-soft offer, not constant pitching.

## Sales / launch sequence

A focused sequence to convert during a promotion or launch window. Higher pitch density, time-bound.

**Typical flow over a launch window:**
1. **Announce** — the offer, the core benefit, the deadline. Clear CTA.
2. **Educate / overcome objection #1** — address the biggest hesitation (price, fit, risk).
3. **Proof** — case studies, testimonials, results.
4. **Overcome objection #2 / FAQ** — handle remaining friction.
5. **Urgency** — deadline approaching, what they'll miss.
6. **Last call** — final hours, direct and short.

Each email: one CTA (to the offer). Lean on real deadlines and real scarcity only — fake urgency burns
trust and, repeated, kills a list.

## Onboarding sequence

For new customers/users. Goal: activation (get them to the value moment) and retention. Often the
difference between a churning trial and a paying customer.

- Map the "aha moment" — the action that correlates with retention — and drive every email toward it.
- One step per email: reduce overwhelm, guide to the next meaningful action.
- Mix instruction with motivation: show the payoff of each step, not just the how.
- Trigger on behavior: completed step → next step; stalled → helpful nudge + offer of help.
- Celebrate progress and the first win.

## Re-engagement / win-back

For subscribers/customers who've gone cold. Goal: reactivate or cleanly remove (list health).

- Acknowledge the silence honestly ("Haven't seen you in a while").
- Offer a reason to return: best content, a strong offer, or simply "still want these emails?"
- Make it easy to stay (one click) or go (easy unsubscribe — protects deliverability).
- After a final attempt with no engagement, suppress/remove — dead weight hurts deliverability and metrics.

## Abandoned cart / abandoned action

Triggered when someone starts but doesn't finish (cart, signup, trial-to-paid).

- **Email 1** (within ~1 hour): remind, remove friction, easy return link.
- **Email 2** (~24 hours): handle the likely objection (shipping, price, doubt), add reassurance/proof.
- **Email 3** (~48–72 hours): final nudge, optional incentive (use sparingly — trains people to abandon).

Keep it helpful, not nagging. Often the abandonment was a distraction, not a decision.

## Newsletters

A recurring relationship, not a sales channel. Goal: consistent value that keeps you welcome in the inbox.

- **Have a clear value promise and format** — readers should know what they get and why it's worth opening.
- **Consistency** — a sustainable cadence held reliably beats sporadic bursts.
- **One main idea per issue** — depth over a cluttered roundup (unless the format IS a curated roundup).
- **Personality** — newsletters live or die on voice; be a person, not a brand bulletin.
- **Occasional, earned asks** — the value-heavy relationship lets you promote without burning goodwill.
- **Measure the right things** — open and click rates, but also replies, forwards, and long-term retention.

## Deliverability & list health

Great copy doesn't matter if it lands in spam.
- **Permission-based only** — send to people who opted in. Purchased lists tank deliverability and may
  break laws (GDPR, CAN-SPAM).
- **Authentication** — SPF, DKIM, DMARC set up (flag this to the user; it's technical setup).
- **Clean the list** — remove hard bounces and long-term non-openers; engagement is a deliverability signal.
- **Avoid spam triggers** — misleading subjects, excessive caps/punctuation/spammy words, image-only emails,
  no unsubscribe link.
- **Compliance** — include a real unsubscribe and a physical address (CAN-SPAM); honor opt-outs promptly;
  meet consent requirements (GDPR) for relevant audiences. Flag legal specifics for the user to verify.
