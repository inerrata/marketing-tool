## Positioning

Positioning is the foundation — it's the space you occupy in the customer's mind relative to alternatives.
Everything else (voice, copy, campaigns) flows from it. Get this right before writing anything downstream.

**Inputs you need:**
- Target customer (specific, not "everyone")
- The category/frame of reference (what bucket do they mentally file you in)
- The key benefit you deliver better than alternatives
- The reason to believe (why your claim is credible)
- The competitive alternative you're winning against (often "doing nothing" or a spreadsheet, not a rival)

**Positioning statement template:**
> For [target customer] who [need or pain], [brand] is the [category] that [key differentiated benefit],
> because [reason to believe]. Unlike [main alternative], [brand] [key difference].

**Strong positioning is:**
- **Different** — if a competitor could make the same claim, it's not positioning, it's a category
  description.
- **Defensible** — backed by something real (technology, model, focus, proof).
- **Relevant** — the differentiation matters to the customer's actual decision.
- **Clear** — a stranger can repeat it after hearing it once.

**Common failure:** positioning on a feature the customer doesn't care about, or on an adjective ("the
easiest") that everyone claims. Position on the dimension where you genuinely win AND the customer
genuinely chooses.

## Value proposition

The value proposition is the headline version of positioning — the single clearest statement of the value
you deliver. It belongs on the homepage hero and in the first line of a pitch.

**Format options:**
- Outcome-led: "We help [who] [achieve outcome] so they can [bigger outcome]."
- Contrast-led: "[Desired outcome], without [the usual pain]."
- Category-led: "The [category] for [specific audience]."

**Test for a strong value prop:**
- Could only your brand (or very few) credibly say it? If anyone could, sharpen it.
- Does it name a specific outcome, not a vague benefit?
- Is it understandable without industry jargon?
- Does it pass the "so what?" test — does the reader immediately grasp why it matters to them?

**Build it from a value map:** list the customer's top jobs-to-be-done, their pains, and their desired
gains. Map your product's pain relievers and gain creators against them. The value prop is built from the
strongest, most differentiated matches.

## Messaging hierarchy

A messaging hierarchy keeps everything consistent — every asset draws from the same well so the brand
sounds coherent across channels.

**Standard structure:**
1. **Tagline** (5–8 words) — memorable, emotional or benefit-driven. Optional clever; never confusing.
2. **Value proposition** (1 sentence) — the clear statement of value (see above).
3. **Core message pillars** (usually 3) — the main themes you consistently reinforce. Each pillar has:
   - A headline (the claim)
   - 1–2 supporting points
   - Proof (stat, example, mechanism)
4. **Elevator pitch** (2–3 sentences) — value prop + lead pillar + proof, conversational.
5. **Boilerplate** (2–3 sentences) — the standard "about" paragraph for bios, footers, press, directories.

Pillars should be distinct (no overlap), each defensible, and together cover the full case for the brand.

## Brand voice & tone

**Voice** is constant (the brand's personality). **Tone** flexes by context (a celebratory tone in an
onboarding email; a measured tone in an outage notice).

**Defining voice — the dimensions approach:**
Pick 3–4 voice dimensions, each on a spectrum, and define where the brand sits and where it doesn't.
For each dimension, write a one-line "we are / we're not" and a do/don't example.

Example voice profile:
- **Direct** (not blunt): We get to the point. We don't bury the lede in qualifiers.
  - Do: "This will cost you about £200/month." Don't: "Pricing varies depending on a range of factors."
- **Warm** (not saccharine): We sound like a helpful human. We don't use corporate distance.
  - Do: "Stuck? Here's the fix." Don't: "Should you require assistance, please consult the following."
- **Confident** (not arrogant): We know our stuff. We don't oversell or trash competitors.
  - Do: "We're the fastest way to do X." Don't: "Everyone else is garbage."
- **Plain-spoken** (not dumbed-down): We avoid jargon. We respect the reader's intelligence.

**Tone guidance grid:** specify how the voice flexes across key moments — celebration, error/apology,
sales, support, bad news. Same personality, adjusted register.

**Practical voice rules to include in a guide:**
- Vocabulary: words we use / words we avoid (and why).
- Grammar/style: contractions? Oxford comma? sentence length? emoji policy?
- Point of view: "you/we" vs third person.
- Reading level target.

## Boilerplate & bios

- **Boilerplate**: who you are, who you help, the proof. Factual, no fluff. Used in press releases, partner
  directories, conference programs.
- **Founder/exec bio**: lead with relevant credibility, one human detail, current role. Versions: 1-line,
  50-word, 150-word.
- **Social bios**: tight value prop + proof + CTA, within character limits. Front-load the differentiator.

## Naming

For products, features, or the brand itself.

**Criteria for a good name:**
- Available (domain, trademark, social handles — flag that the user must verify legal availability)
- Pronounceable and spellable on first hearing
- Distinctive in the category
- Not boxing you in (too-literal names limit expansion)
- Free of unintended meanings in relevant languages/markets

**Naming approaches:** descriptive (Salesforce), suggestive/metaphor (Amazon), abstract/coined (Kodak),
founder/origin, compound. Generate options across approaches, then filter against the criteria.

**Always note:** name availability and trademark must be legally verified by the user; this skill suggests
directions, it doesn't clear names.

## Message testing

Before committing messaging, pressure-test it:
- **The repeat test:** can someone repeat your value prop after hearing it once?
- **The competitor test:** could a competitor say the same thing? If yes, it's not differentiated.
- **The "so what" test:** chase each claim with "so what?" until you hit something the customer truly cares
  about.
- **The skeptic test:** would a skeptical buyer believe it without proof? If not, add proof or soften.
- **The grandmother test (for B2C) / the busy-exec test (for B2B):** is it clear to someone outside the
  industry / to someone with 5 seconds?
