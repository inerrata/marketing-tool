## Foundational frameworks

Pick the framework that fits the format and the temperature of the audience (cold = doesn't know you;
warm = knows you, hasn't bought; hot = ready to buy).

**PAS — Problem, Agitation, Solution.** Best default for cold audiences and short-to-medium copy. Name the
problem, make the reader feel its cost, then present the solution.
- Problem: "Your team wastes Friday afternoons reconciling spreadsheets."
- Agitation: "That's four hours a week, fifty hours a quarter, gone — and one fat-finger away from a
  reporting error that lands on your desk."
- Solution: "Acme reconciles them automatically, every night, with an audit trail."

**AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action.** Best for longer formats (sales pages, long ads) and warm
audiences. Grab attention, build interest with relevance, create desire with proof and outcome, drive action.

**BAB — Before, After, Bridge.** Best for transformation-led offers. Paint the current painful state, paint
the desired future state, position the product as the bridge between them.

**4 Ps — Promise, Picture, Proof, Push.** Good for emails and ads. Make a promise, help them picture it,
prove it's real, push them to act.

**FAB — Features, Advantages, Benefits.** Use to translate a feature list into copy. For each feature, ask
"so what?" until you reach a benefit the reader actually cares about.
- Feature: "256-bit encryption" → Advantage: "data is unreadable if intercepted" → Benefit: "you pass your
  client's security audit without a six-week remediation project."

## Headlines

The headline does 80% of the work; most readers decide whether to continue based on it alone.

**Reliable headline formulas:**
- Outcome + audience + removed obstacle: "Ship features faster without breaking production."
- Specific number/result: "How we cut churn 31% in one quarter."
- How-to: "How to [achieve desired outcome] (even if [common obstacle])."
- Counterintuitive/contrarian: "Stop A/B testing your headlines. Do this instead."
- Question that hits a real pain: "Still copy-pasting data between tools?"
- Direct benefit: "Invoicing that takes five minutes, not five hours."

**Rules:**
- Be specific. "Increase revenue" is weak; "Add £40k MRR in 90 days" is strong (if true).
- One idea per headline.
- Front-load the most important word; readers scan the first 2–3 words.
- Write 10, keep 1. Quantity first, then ruthless selection.
- Test the headline against the promise: can the body actually deliver what the headline claims? If not,
  the headline lies and will hurt you.

## Email copy

**Marketing/cold email structure:**
1. **Subject line** — under ~50 characters. Either a curiosity gap or a specific, relevant benefit. Avoid
   spam triggers (ALL CAPS, "free!!!", excessive punctuation). Test against: would *I* open this?
2. **Preview text** — the snippet shown after the subject. Complement the subject, don't repeat it. This is
   free real estate most people waste.
3. **Opening line** — no throat-clearing. Skip "I hope this email finds you well" and "I'm reaching out
   because." Open with the reader's world, a specific observation, or the payoff.
4. **Body** — one idea. For cold/marketing email keep to ~3 short paragraphs, scannable, with line breaks.
   For nurture email, value first; sell second or not at all.
5. **CTA** — exactly one ask. Make it low-friction and specific ("Grab a 15-min slot" beats "Let me know
   your thoughts").
6. **P.S.** — optional but high-read. Use for the strongest secondary hook or a deadline.

**Subject line angles to generate variants from:** curiosity, specific benefit, urgency/scarcity (only if
real), social proof, personalization, question, pattern interrupt.

**Cold email specifics:** Relevance is everything. The first line must prove you know who they are. Keep the
whole thing under ~120 words. One CTA. Make the ask proportional to the relationship (don't ask for 30
minutes from a stranger — ask for permission or a yes/no).

## Landing pages & sales pages

A landing page has ONE goal and removes everything that doesn't serve it (including the main nav, often).

**Standard high-converting structure:**
1. **Hero** — headline (the outcome), subheadline (how/for whom), primary CTA, and ideally a hero
   visual/proof. The reader should understand what you offer and why it matters in under 5 seconds.
2. **Social proof bar** — logos, ratings, or a key stat, immediately under the hero to build trust early.
3. **Problem** — name the pain clearly and specifically. Make the reader feel understood.
4. **Solution** — introduce the product as the answer. Tie features to outcomes (use FAB).
5. **How it works** — 3 steps, reduce perceived effort and risk.
6. **Proof** — testimonials (specific results, not "great product!"), case studies, data, before/after.
7. **Objection handling / FAQ** — surface the real reasons people hesitate and answer them.
8. **Offer clarity** — pricing, what's included, guarantee/risk reversal.
9. **Final CTA** — repeat the primary action with a reason to act now.

**Sales page (longer) adds:** deeper story, more proof stacking, a clear "who this is / isn't for"
section, bonus/urgency mechanics, and often a longer guarantee section. Length should match price and
complexity — high-consideration purchases justify long pages.

**CTA discipline:** one primary action repeated; secondary actions (if any) visually subordinate.

## Homepages

Different from a landing page: a homepage serves multiple audiences and intents, and keeps navigation.

**Above the fold must answer in 5 seconds:** What is this? Who is it for? Why should I care? What do I do
next?

**Structure:**
1. Hero: clear value proposition headline (not a clever tagline nobody understands), subhead, primary CTA,
   secondary CTA (e.g. "See how it works").
2. Social proof.
3. Core value pillars (usually 3) — the main reasons to care, each benefit-led.
4. Product demonstration (visual or short explainer).
5. Deeper proof / case studies.
6. Segmented paths if you serve distinct audiences ("For teams" / "For individuals").
7. Final CTA + footer.

Avoid the most common homepage failure: a vague aspirational headline ("Empowering the future of work")
that says nothing. Lead with clarity; you can be clever once you've been clear.

## Ads (paid)

Constraints are tight, so every word fights for its place.

**Search ads:** match the searcher's intent and include the keyword. Lead with the benefit or the answer to
their query. Use the description to add proof and a CTA. Include extensions (sitelinks, callouts) where the
platform allows.

**Social/display ads:** the scroll-stopper is the visual + first line. The first line should hook on
pain, curiosity, or a bold specific claim. Copy length depends on platform and temperature — cold
audiences need more context; retargeting can be short and direct. Always have one clear CTA.

**Ad copy angles to test:** problem-led, benefit-led, social proof, urgency/offer, curiosity,
testimonial-led, objection-led ("No credit card required").

**Compliance:** no unsubstantiated claims, no fake urgency, follow platform policies (health, finance, and
"before/after" claims are heavily restricted).

## Social posts

**Hook first.** The first line determines whether the rest is read. Strong hook types: bold/contrarian
claim, surprising number, relatable frustration, curiosity gap, "I was wrong about X."

**Structure for value posts:** Hook → context/story → insight/value → takeaway → (optional soft CTA).

**Per-platform notes:**
- **LinkedIn:** professional but human. Short lines, white space, a hook that earns the "...see more"
  click. Story + lesson performs well.
- **X/Twitter:** punchy, one idea, threads for depth. Lead the thread with the payoff.
- **Instagram:** visual-led; caption supports the image. First line hooks before the "more" cutoff.
- **TikTok/Reels:** the hook is the first 1–2 seconds of the video; the caption is secondary.

Avoid engagement-bait and hollow "agree?" prompts — they erode credibility.

## Product descriptions

- Lead with the benefit, support with the feature. "Stays cold for 24 hours — double-wall vacuum
  insulation" beats listing specs cold.
- Write for the buyer's decision criteria, not an internal feature checklist.
- Use sensory and concrete language for physical products; use outcome language for software.
- Include the practical specifics buyers actually need (size, compatibility, what's in the box) — clarity
  reduces returns and support tickets.
- Scannable: short paragraph + bulleted specifics.

## CTAs

The CTA is where intent converts to action. Weak CTAs leak conversions.

- **Be specific and outcome-oriented:** "Get my free audit" beats "Submit." "Start my 14-day trial" beats
  "Sign up."
- **First person can lift conversions:** "Start my trial" vs "Start your trial" — worth testing.
- **Reduce friction in the surrounding copy:** "No credit card. Cancel anytime." beside the button.
- **One primary CTA per view.** Competing CTAs split attention and lower conversion.
- **Match the CTA to the temperature:** cold traffic → low-commitment ask (learn more, get the guide);
  hot traffic → high-commitment ask (buy, book).

## Microcopy

The small text that shapes experience and trust: form labels, button states, error messages, empty
states, tooltips, confirmation messages.

- Be human and clear, not robotic. "Hmm, that password doesn't match" beats "Error 422."
- Reassure at moments of friction (payment, sign-up): say what happens next and what won't happen
  ("We'll never share your email").
- Confirmation copy is a brand moment — make it warm and clear, and set the next expectation.

## Editing checklist

Run every piece of copy through this before delivering:

1. Is there ONE clear message and ONE clear action?
2. Does it lead with the reader's problem/desire, not the product?
3. Is every vague adjective replaced with a specific claim or number?
4. Is every significant claim backed by proof?
5. Active voice? Filler cut? Adverbs cut?
6. Does each line earn the next?
7. Does the headline's promise match what the body delivers?
8. Read aloud — any stumbles, any line that sounds like a robot or a brochure?
9. Is it the right length for the format and audience temperature — nothing padded, nothing missing?
10. Would the actual target reader find this relevant, credible, and worth acting on?
