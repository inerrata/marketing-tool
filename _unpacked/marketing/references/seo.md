## Search intent

Intent is the most important SEO concept for content. Match the content type to what the searcher actually
wants, or it won't rank no matter how well-written.

**Four intent types:**
- **Informational** — "how to," "what is," "why does." Wants to learn. → guides, explainers, how-tos.
- **Navigational** — searching for a specific brand/site. → your branded pages.
- **Commercial investigation** — "best," "vs," "review," "top." Comparing before buying. → comparisons,
  reviews, listicles, "best X for Y."
- **Transactional** — "buy," "pricing," "discount," "near me." Ready to act. → product/pricing/local pages.

**To determine intent:** search the keyword and look at what currently ranks. Google has already decided
what intent it serves. If the top results are all listicles, a single-product page won't break in. Match
the dominant format.

## Keyword strategy

- **Head terms** (high volume, broad, competitive) vs. **long-tail** (lower volume, specific, easier to
  rank, often higher intent). Early on, win long-tail; build toward head terms.
- **Topic clusters:** a pillar page targets a broad term; cluster pages target related long-tail terms and
  link back to the pillar. This builds topical authority and ranks better than scattered one-offs.
- **Prioritize keywords by:** relevance to your offer, intent (commercial/transactional convert better),
  realistic difficulty vs. your site's authority, and volume. A low-volume, high-intent, low-difficulty
  term often beats a high-volume vanity term.
- Note: this skill recommends keyword *strategy and structure*; actual volume and difficulty data require
  a tool (Ahrefs, Semrush, etc.) — flag when the user should pull real numbers rather than estimating.

## Content brief structure

A brief is the spec a writer (human or AI) follows to produce a piece that ranks and converts.

**Brief template:**
1. **Target keyword** (+ secondary keywords) and, if known, volume/difficulty.
2. **Search intent** and the content type that matches it.
3. **Working title** — includes primary keyword, under ~60 characters, compelling not just optimized.
4. **Meta description** — 150–160 chars, includes keyword, includes a reason to click (benefit/CTA).
5. **Target word count** — based on what's ranking (analyze top results; don't pad to hit a number).
6. **Outline** — H2s and H3s covering the subtopics searchers and competitors address. Structure around
   the questions the searcher needs answered.
7. **Questions to answer** — pull from "People Also Ask" and related searches; these capture intent.
8. **Differentiation angle** — what will make this better than what currently ranks? (original data, deeper
   how-to, clearer structure, expert insight, better examples). Ranking #1 requires being meaningfully
   better, not just present.
9. **Internal links** — which existing pages to link to/from.
10. **Primary CTA** — what action the piece should drive (don't write traffic that doesn't convert).
11. **E-E-A-T signals to include** — author expertise, sources, first-hand experience, data.

## On-page optimization

- **Primary keyword placement:** title, H1, first ~100 words, at least one H2, naturally throughout, and
  in the meta. Never stuff — write for humans first; awkward keyword repetition hurts both ranking and
  readability.
- **Use semantic/related terms** (entities and subtopics Google associates with the topic), not just exact
  keyword repetition. Modern search rewards comprehensive topical coverage.
- **Structure for scannability:** descriptive headings, short paragraphs, lists, bold key points. Both
  readers and search engines reward clear structure.
- **Optimize media:** descriptive file names and alt text (also an accessibility necessity).
- **Answer the query fast:** especially for informational intent, give the core answer early (featured-
  snippet-friendly), then go deep.

## Title & meta

- **Title tag:** under ~60 chars (or it truncates in results). Lead with the keyword/benefit. Make it the
  most clickable accurate option among the results — clickthrough rate influences ranking.
- **Meta description:** 150–160 chars. Not a direct ranking factor, but drives clicks. Include the keyword
  (it bolds in results) and a clear reason to click.

## Content quality for ranking

Google rewards content that satisfies the searcher better than alternatives. Practically:
- **Comprehensive:** cover the topic fully enough that the searcher doesn't need another result.
- **Original value:** first-hand experience, original data, a unique angle, or genuinely better
  explanation. Rehashed content that adds nothing struggles.
- **E-E-A-T** (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust): demonstrate real experience, cite
  credible sources, show who wrote it and why they're credible. Especially critical for YMYL (your money
  or your life — health, finance, legal) topics.
- **Freshness:** for topics that change, keep content updated; stale content decays.
- **Engagement:** content that holds attention (clear, well-structured, useful) sends positive signals.

## Internal linking

- Link new content to relevant existing pages and vice versa — spreads authority and helps both ranking
  and navigation.
- Use descriptive anchor text (the linked words should describe the destination, not "click here").
- Build topic clusters: cluster content links up to the pillar; the pillar links down to clusters.
- Link from high-authority pages to pages you want to boost.
