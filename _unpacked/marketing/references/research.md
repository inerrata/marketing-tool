## Audience personas / ICP

A persona makes the abstract "audience" concrete enough to write to. An ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) is the
B2B sibling — the profile of the company + buyer most likely to buy and succeed.

**Persona structure:**
- **Snapshot** — name/label, role or life situation, the one-line "who they are."
- **Demographics/firmographics** — B2C: age, situation, income bracket if relevant. B2B: role, seniority,
  company size, industry, tech stack.
- **Goals** — what they're trying to achieve (professionally and/or personally).
- **Pains/frustrations** — what's blocking them, what they complain about, what wakes them at 3am.
- **Objections** — why they might hesitate or not buy (price, risk, switching cost, skepticism, internal
  politics).
- **Buying triggers** — the events or feelings that make them act now.
- **Information sources / watering holes** — where they go to learn, who they trust, which platforms,
  communities, publications, influencers.
- **Decision role** (B2B) — are they the user, the buyer, the economic decision-maker, a gatekeeper?

**Build personas from evidence where possible** — customer interviews, support tickets, sales calls,
reviews, analytics — not just imagination. Flag clearly when a persona is hypothesis vs. evidence-based.

**Avoid the common trap:** demographic detail that doesn't change how you market. "Drives a Subaru, likes
hiking" is useless unless it informs the message. Prioritize goals, pains, objections, and triggers — those
drive copy.

## Segmentation

When the audience isn't monolithic, segment so messaging can be tailored.

**Segmentation bases:**
- Needs/jobs (most useful for messaging) — group by what they're trying to accomplish.
- Behavior — usage, purchase history, engagement, lifecycle stage.
- Firmographic/demographic — size, industry, role / age, situation.
- Value — high vs. low value, or high vs. low intent.

**Prioritize segments by:** size, fit (likelihood to buy and succeed), reachability, and value. Focus on
the highest-priority segment(s) first rather than diluting across all.

## Jobs to be done

A powerful lens: customers "hire" a product to do a job. Understanding the job reveals the real competition
and the real value.

- **Functional job:** the practical task ("reconcile my accounts").
- **Emotional job:** how they want to feel ("feel in control, not anxious at month-end").
- **Social job:** how they want to be perceived ("look competent to my boss").

Frame value around the full job, not just the functional task. The competition is anything that does the
job — including spreadsheets, manual workarounds, and doing nothing.

## Competitor analysis

**For each competitor, capture:**
- **Positioning** — what do they claim to be? (read their homepage hero, tagline, about page)
- **Target audience** — who are they clearly built for?
- **Key messages** — their core claims and pillars.
- **Tone/voice** — how do they communicate?
- **Offer & pricing** — what and how much (if visible).
- **Proof** — what credibility do they lean on (logos, stats, testimonials)?
- **Channels** — where are they active and visible?
- **Strengths** — what they do well.
- **Gaps/weaknesses** — what they're NOT saying, who they're NOT serving, where they're vulnerable.

**The output that matters:** the **gap analysis**. Where is there white space — an audience underserved, a
message no one's making, a tone no one's using, a job no one's solving? That gap is your positioning
opportunity. Competitor analysis is only useful if it ends in "therefore we should..."

**Categorize competitors:** direct (same solution), indirect (different solution, same job), and
substitutes (DIY, doing nothing). Don't ignore the last two — they're often the real competition.

## Positioning audit

When auditing your own (or a client's) positioning against the market:
1. Map all players on 2 axes that matter to customers (e.g. price vs. specialization, or ease vs. power).
2. Identify where everyone clusters (crowded, hard to differentiate) and where there's open space.
3. Assess where the brand currently sits vs. where it could credibly own space.
4. Recommend a position that is differentiated, defensible, relevant, and clear.

## Voice-of-customer research

The best marketing copy uses the customer's own language. Mine it from:
- **Reviews** (yours and competitors') — the exact words people use for pains and gains.
- **Support tickets & sales calls** — recurring questions, objections, and "aha" moments.
- **Community/forum threads** — how people describe the problem unprompted.
- **Survey open-text responses** — especially "what almost stopped you from buying?"

Capture verbatim phrases and feed them into copy — headlines, objection handling, FAQs. Marketing that
echoes the customer's own words feels instantly more relevant and credible than internal jargon.
